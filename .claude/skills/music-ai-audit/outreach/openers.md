# Cold-outreach opener bank

A library of opener patterns for the £750 AI Workflow Audit, for the drafting step in
`../../audit-prospect-research/SKILL.md` (step 5) and methods 5 and 7 of `playbook.md`
(LinkedIn/email outreach and targeted DMs). Mined from a generic US agency prompt pack and
rewritten for Chris's voice — the hype, "warfare" framing and manipulation tactics are stripped
out; only the honest structures survive.

**These are patterns, not send-ready copy.** Every draft still needs one line of genuine
specificity per prospect — something true from their site, not flattery — and Chris's review
before anything goes out.

## House rules (same as the playbook)

- **Draft only.** Chris approves and sends every message. No armed sequences, no auto-send.
- **Name-only greeting** — "Sarah," not "Hi Sarah,". UK spelling, GBP, no emojis.
- **Calm, not salesy.** No fake urgency, no "god-level", no borrowed Liberty/BBC credibility, no
  invented client counts or results.
- **Conversation first on cold.** Don't pitch the £750 in message one to a stranger — open a
  conversation, offer the free 15-minute mini-audit, let the full audit come later.
- **One true detail beats ten adjectives.** If you can't find a real, specific thing about their
  business, the prospect isn't researched enough to write to yet.
- **Short.** If it's over ~90 words, cut it. The two-line version usually outperforms.

## Subject lines (email track)

Under six words, lower-case-ish, no spam words ("free", "guarantee", "£££", all-caps). Aim for a
real observation, not a hook. Good shapes:
- "quick thought on [company]'s [thing]"
- "[specific admin task] at [company]"
- "the enquiry-reply gap"
- "15 minutes, one AI tool"

On a warm/referral thread, no subject cleverness needed — the mutual name does the work.

## The patterns

Ranked roughly by fit for the audit. Pick by what you actually know about the prospect.

### 1. The observation opener (default for cold)
*When you've found one real, specific time-sink on their site.* This is the honest cousin of the
"problem-first" script — lead with something true you noticed, not a pain you're guessing at.

> Sarah — saw [label] is putting out a release most weeks. That's a lot of the same admin round
> each one — assets, links, submissions. I help small music businesses hand that repetitive stuff
> to AI tools. Not pitching anything — what's the bit of a release week you'd most like off your
> plate?

Music variant swaps in release/campaign admin; Brighton variant swaps in enquiry replies, booking,
quotes. The close is always a question, never a calendar link.

### 2. The audit offer (most on-model)
*When you want to lead with the actual offer — warm-ish prospects, or a referral warm-up.* This is
the £750 audit's natural opener: a specific, bounded free thing with a defined deliverable.

> Mark — I run free 15-minute AI audits for owner-run businesses: I look at where your week goes
> and show you one tool that'd save real time, no charge and no obligation. Noticed [specific
> thing]. Worth a quick look for [company]?

Keep the free thing genuinely free and genuinely useful even if they never buy. The full £750 audit
is what you mention *on that call*, not in the email.

### 3. The value-first opener
*When you can give something useful up front with nothing asked back.* Reciprocity done honestly —
the tip has to be real and specific to them.

> Priya — one quick thing: [company]'s contact form goes to a shared inbox, and those are where
> enquiries quietly die. A £0 setup with [tool] auto-acknowledges every one within a minute — worth
> doing whether or not we ever speak. I do this kind of thing for a living; happy to share two more
> if useful.

### 4. The hours-back opener (light ROI)
*When you can make an honest, rough estimate — not a fabricated dollar figure.* The US version
invents a precise "$X in 90 days"; don't. Frame it as time, hedged.

> Tom — rough maths: if [company] handles ~30 booking enquiries a week by hand, that's the best
> part of a day gone. Most of it's automatable with off-the-shelf tools. I map exactly which ones
> in a free 15-minute audit — want me to take a look?

Never promise a number you can't stand behind. "The best part of a day" is honest; "£4,000/month"
is not, unless it genuinely is.

### 5. The two-line opener
*When brevity fits — busy owners, DMs, a crowded inbox.* Often the highest reply rate. One
observation, one question, nothing else.

> Jen — who handles the admin round each release at [label] right now? Asking because most of it's
> automatable and I help music businesses do exactly that.

> Dan — what eats the most time in a week at [company]? I sort that kind of thing with AI for
> owner-run businesses locally.

### 6. The voice-note / Loom opener
*Chris's edge — a personalised 60–90-second video or voice message beats text.* The playbook already
flags this (method 5). The written line just frames the recording.

> Sarah — recorded you a quick 90-second video (below): had a look at [company]'s release flow and
> spotted a couple of things AI could take off you. No pitch, just useful. Worth a watch?

Video script shape: 0–10s who you are + why them specifically ("four years pitching radio, so I know
where a music week goes"); 10–70s screen-share the one real thing you noticed; last 20s the free
15-minute audit as the next step. Genuine, unpolished, one take.

### 7. The proof-drop opener (once there's a case study)
*Only when you have a real, permissioned result — never before.* This is the case-study skill's
output doing outreach work. No result, don't use this pattern.

> Alex — helped [named or anonymised similar business] claw back about [honest figure] a week by
> handing their [task] to a couple of AI tools. [Company] looks like it's in a similar spot — want
> to see roughly how?

Pairs with `../case-study/` output. Anonymise if consent wasn't given; never imply a result you
didn't produce.

### 8. The referral-partner warm-up
*Not a prospect opener — a partner one.* Kept here because it feeds the same pool. Lives in full at
playbook method 2; the short version:

> Ben — I do AI automation for [music / Brighton] businesses. If a client of yours ever asks about
> AI or moans about admin, I'd be glad to be your go-to — and happy to pass back a cut of anything
> that lands. Worth a quick chat?

## Follow-up (calm, not the US seven-touch blitz)

The source pack pushes a seven-email, forty-five-day sequence. That's too much for Chris's tone and
list size. Use **three touches, then stop** — a no is fine, and silence after three is a no.

1. **Touch 1 — the opener** (any pattern above).
2. **Touch 2 (about 4 days later) — a different angle, not a nudge.** Add one new useful thing;
   don't say "just bumping this".
   > Sarah — one more: [second specific tip or observation]. Still happy to do the free 15 minutes
   > whenever suits.
3. **Touch 3 (about a week after that) — the clean close.** Give them an easy out; it often gets
   the reply.
   > Sarah — I'll leave it there so I'm not cluttering your inbox. If the admin ever gets loud,
   > you know where I am. All the best with the [release / season].

Log every outcome (`sent / replied / call booked / dead`) back into the batch file so the next
batch learns which pattern and which sector actually reply.

## What was deliberately left out

From the source pack, and why: the "fake referral" name-drop (dishonest), "compliment + critique" of
a stranger (presumptuous, off-brand), "pattern interrupt / neuroscience" gimmicks (hype), fabricated
ROI figures, and manufactured urgency. None of them fit an honest, consultative offer — and the
straight versions convert better with the businesses Chris actually wants.
